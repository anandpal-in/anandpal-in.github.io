Who Owns Publicly Funded Research?

Global Shifts in Scientific Publishing and India’s Strategic Choices

✍️ Dr. Anand Pal 📅 June 26, 2026 ⏱️ 10–12 min read

For decades, one question has remained surprisingly unresolved:

Who owns knowledge created with public money?

If the public finances scientific research through taxes, should the public be required to pay again to read the results?

Traditionally, the answer has been YES. Researchers conduct the work using public funds, universities provide the infrastructure, reviewers evaluate manuscripts without compensation, and publishers ultimately own the final published version. Libraries, governments, and even the authors themselves often pay subscription fees or article processing charges (APCs) to access or publish that research.

This paradox has quietly shaped academic publishing for decades, but recent policy developments suggest that governments are beginning to rethink the model. China has announced plans to reduce spending on expensive article processing charges, while the proposed U.S. Fiscal Year 2027 budget questions why public money should be used both to publish and later access taxpayer-funded research.

The Rise of the Metrics Economy

Alongside the publishing debate, another transformation has taken place within academia. Citation counts, impact factors, journal quartiles, H-index values, and institutional rankings increasingly influence hiring, promotion, and research funding. Originally designed as evaluation tools, these metrics have gradually become objectives in themselves.

Metrics undoubtedly provide useful information, but when researchers begin optimizing for numbers rather than discovery, incentives shift. Scientific success risks being measured more by visibility than by originality or long-term impact.

Science advances through curiosity, reproducibility, and open criticism—not through optimization of bibliometric indicators.

The Commercialization of Open Access

Open access emerged from an admirable principle: research should be freely accessible to everyone. However, much of the commercial publishing ecosystem has shifted the financial burden from readers to authors through article processing charges (APCs). While access has improved for readers, publication has become increasingly expensive for researchers and funding agencies.

This author-pays model has created incentives where publication volume and journal prestige often become commercial assets. The concern is therefore not open access itself, but the commercialization of scholarly communication.

Key Question

If taxpayers finance research, should they also finance publishing fees and then pay again to access the same research?

Beyond Metrics

The modern academic ecosystem increasingly resembles social media. Online platforms reward impressions, followers, and engagement. Academia rewards citations, impact factors, and rankings. Both metrics carry useful information, but neither should be mistaken for intrinsic quality.

Researchers naturally respond to incentives. Universities reward publications, funding agencies reward citation performance, and journals compete for prestige. Over time, the pursuit of knowledge can quietly become the pursuit of measurable visibility.

Do We Really Need Expensive Journals?

The success of arXiv demonstrates that rapid dissemination of high-quality research does not necessarily require expensive publication models. Since its launch in 1991, arXiv has become an indispensable platform for physics, mathematics, computer science, and several other disciplines, enabling researchers to share findings long before formal journal publication.

This does not diminish the importance of peer review or journals. Rather, it shows that dissemination, peer review, and long-term archiving need not always be tied to costly commercial publishing models.

A National Research Infrastructure for India

India's One Nation, One Subscription initiative represents a significant investment in improving access to global research. However, access alone should not be the long-term objective. India has an opportunity to build sovereign scientific infrastructure that complements international publishing.

What could this include?

  • A national preprint repository covering all disciplines.
  • Government-funded Diamond Open Access journals charging neither authors nor readers.
  • AI-assisted editorial screening to improve efficiency and reduce publication costs.
  • Open and transparent peer review where appropriate.
  • Persistent digital identifiers and long-term preservation of publicly funded research.
  • Integration across universities, funding agencies, institutional repositories, and national laboratories.

Scientific publishing should increasingly be viewed as strategic national infrastructure, much like digital payments, artificial intelligence, or semiconductor manufacturing. Countries that aspire to lead in science should also help shape how scientific knowledge is communicated, preserved, and shared.

Returning to the Purpose of Research

Scientific publishing exists because science exists—not the other way around.

Metrics remain useful. Journals remain valuable. Peer review remains essential. But none of these should become ends in themselves.

The central question is not who publishes research. The real question is who ultimately owns knowledge created with public money.

If taxpayers fund discovery, knowledge should remain a public good—not merely another commercial asset.
